Grapes

All wine starts with the grapes

There are over 10,000 grape varieties in the world from which people make wine. The ones listed here are a very, very, small sampling of that selection. Below you will find what we call the 18 Noble Grapes, which are the most widely planted grapes, globally. Most of them are French. I'm not 100% sure of who decided that these were the Nobelest of Grapes when some really awesome grapes, like Mouvedre/Monastrell, Carménère, Torrontes, not to mention all of the grapes which are idigenous to different countries that also make world class wine, but don't bother with the French stuff, but I digress. For our purposes we had to limit the scope to these varietals. If you want to learn more about grapes you don't see listed here, check out the Regions page. The specific descriptions aren't as comprehensive but we can talk about wines from a broader selection of grapes. For now, click on a heading below to explore these grapes further and you'll be on your way to deciphering unfamiliar wine labels in no time!

Reds
  • Pinot Noir

    One of the lightest red wines around (although some of the high octane ones from California and other warm weather climates might have you believing otherwise) Pinot Noir is the grape that is among the most expensive wine in the world, Domaine de la Romanée-Conti, aka DRC. As a grape Pinot Noir is very finicky, also contributing to its increased cost. French Pinot Noir comes from a region called Burgundy. Burgundies are light, acidic reds that can have a bit of earthy funk to them. Generally you'll find notes of cherry and other red fruits usually accompanied with a light woodiness. In the United States California and Oregon are the kings of Pinot, with California making some fuller, riper expressions, while Oregon, with its cooler climate produces lighter more quintessential examples (no shade intended on California, it's just different.) When you look at a map Willamette Valley in Oregon is on almost the same latitude as Burgundy, potentially contributing to their similar character. Pinot Noirs are great food wines because they're generally not bold enough to overpower the flavors of whatever you're eating.

  • Grenache

    I love Grenache. Easy drinking light-medium bodied wines Grenache is very versatile: great for food and sipping. Grenache from France mostly comes from the Rhone region os southern France. It is generally on the lighter side of medium, and fruity with notes of juicy berries sometimes accompanied by a slight green undertone (like if someone put a sprig of herb garnish in your cocktail). If it's from a warmer climate, either a warmer region or just a warmer than average growing season, Grenache can have heavier pepper notes, while cooler examples are a bit more floral. In Spain Grenache is called Garnacha, tends to be more solidly medium bodied than French versions and usually has these peppery notes, Spanish Garnacha is one of the best wines you can buy in the $10 range; unoaked varieties make a killer Sangria.

  • Merlot

    Merlot was right up there with California Cabernet before Paul Giamatti is the movie Sideways DESTROYED its reputation with one like "I'm not drinking any [forking] Merlot!" Merlot is very similar to Cabernet in many ways and is usually cheaper to boot. It's not quite as intense as Cab but has many of the same intense red, and purple, fruits, softer vanilla, and espresso or mocha notes from the oak, with less intense pepper (usually.) Additionally, because of its artificially tarnished reputation quality Merlot is usually cheaper than similarly revered Cab. Many people even think they're drinking a slight lighter bodied Cab when you give them Merlot! So forget Sideways, if you like Cab, find a quality bottle of California/Washington Merlot or Merlot dominant Bordeaux Blends (ask the clerk at your neighborhood wine shop for something from the Right Bank or inexpensive "vin de table" aka table wine (because it's cheaper to produce, cheaper wines from Bordeaux are generally more heavily Bordeaux than Cab, but this is not a rule.) Fun fact: the wine Paul Giamatti drinks at the end of the movie, Cheval Blanc, is a revered Merlot based wine.

  • Sangiovese

    Sangiovese is the quintessential Italian wine grape. It’s a medium bodied, very tannic grape that can have notes of cherry or blackberry as well as spices as anise and licorice depending on the style of production. Sangiovese is a, if not the, major player in Tuscan red wines. Brunello di Montalcino is one of the most sought after and highly regarding Italian wines and is always (by law) 100% Sangiovese. Rosso di Montalcino, Brunello’s little cousin is also 100% Sangiovese and Hannibal Lecter’s favorite, Chianti, always has some amount of Sangiovese. Although some modern styles of Sangiovese can be more fruit forward, especially the odd one here or there you find from California, or other places that are not Italy, they tend to be much earthier with notes of earth, mushroom, and those barnyard, gamey, Brettanomyces funky flavors.

  • Nebbiolo

    Nebbiolo is the other major heavy hitter in the Italian wine world. In Italian wine, some people will tell you, it’s all about the three B’s: Brunello (with is Sangiovese) and Barolo and Barbaresco which are both made with Nebbiolo. Nebbiolo is mostly closely associated with the Piedmont region in Northwestern Italy. Nebbiolo is medium bodied with purple fruit notes such as blackberry and plum. Like most Italian wines Nebbiolo wines are usually in a more traditional style with that quintessential old-world funk.Nebbiolo isn’t quite as acidic as Sangiovese but it is also highly tannic. Italian wines can almost always benefit from a moderate to significant amount of aging (think about ten years, to start.) If you find that you like Barolo and Barbaresco, but don’t like spending upwards of $50 for every bottle of wine, try Langhe Nebbiolo, which is the same grape, from a slightly less prestigious region. High quality producers of Barolo and Barbaresco almost always also make Nebbiolo wines from Langhe, and they have been called baby Barolos and Barbarescos, at a much more palatable price.

  • Tempranillo

    Moving away from the French and Italian varietals (finally) Tempranillo is a Spanish grape that makes fuller bodied wines. Spain’s Rioja region is famous for Tempranillo as it is the major component in these big red wines. Tempranillo’s characteristics can change pretty dramatically depending on how long it has been aged in oak (Crianza has spent the least time in oak, Gran Reserva the most.) If you want a spicy, smokey expression that will give good California Cabernet a run for its money (without the tendency towards sweet fruit, jammy overtones) try a Rioja. Crianza Rioja will be a little brighter with more dark cherries and even some berries coming through, with a very slight green flavor, and woodsiness from the brief oak aging. Other regions in the world grow Tempranillo in small amounts, but the vast majority of my experience with the grape is from Spain. I have had a handful of California experiments and they are some funky, interesting wines, but not necessarily for the faint of heart.

  • Cabernet Sauvignon

    If you want a full bodied red wine that will punch you in the face with oaky, vanilla, pepper and smoke notes, try a California Cab. If you can't afford a good bottle from California (starting around $30) try some from Argentina, Chile, or Washington State, where you can get a similar style for $20 or less. Cabernet is the mostly widely planted grape in the world because vintners can charge the most for them. If you know nothing about wine you've probably heard of Cabernet Sauvignon because it seems like everybody's dad drinks it. Cabernet is what put California wine on the map (look up "The Judgement of Paris" to learn about how Robert Mondavi embarrassed a bunch of French wine connoisseurs.) Cabernet is one of the big, defining, grapes of France's Bordeaux region. In a Bordeaux blend (any blend of the five main grapes in Bordeaux: Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Malbec, Cabernet Franc, and Petit Verdot) you'll get a much less intense version than you find in the new world. Bordeuax have a funky , gamey, "barnyard" flavor courtesy of a specific yeast called Brettanomyces (aka Brett.) Usually Cabernet is way too intense for me to have with food so I often sip on it while cooking and will have something else while I'm eating.

  • Syrah

    You've probably seen the words Shiraz on a bottle of wine from Australia, perhaps with a certain marsupial on the label. Syrah and Shiraz are the same same grape. Syrah can be a big fruit and pepper bomb, a big red-purple wine with earthy notes of olive and menthol or a lighter medium bodied fruit forward wine, depending on where it's grown. Australia is particularly known for its Syrah, but don't let those big bottles of kangaroo branded wine fool you; Syrah isn't meant to blow out your pallet, and smaller producers in Australia have been working hard over the last 10+ years to restore their reputation for quality that was damaged when the market was flooded with these cheap big brands. Washington State also makes some intense Syrah, usually on the earthier end of the spectrum. Syrah is originally from France (are you noticing a pattern here?) if you like a big, bold, fruity wine you'll probably like Syrah, try some from Chile for a bargain that doesn't compromise on quality, then see what Australia is really made of when you're ready to spend a little more.

  • Malbec

    Full disclosure: half of my family is from Argentina, so I have had more than my fair share of this grape. Malbec is originally from Bordeaux, in France, but almost went extinct before blowing up the Argentine wine scene. Malbec can be a medium bodied wine, less intense than Cabernet, but traditionally is a big purple wine. Malbec has gotten a reputation as an easy, everyman, red wine, that is perhaps not as intimidating as other red varietals. In Argentina there are two main styles of Malbec: fruity/jammy and smokey/earthy. All Malbec can have notes of plum, blackberries, cassis and dark cherries. Fruit-froward, jammy, styles may have milder vanilla oak notes, whereas earthier versions will have definite notes of dirt, wood, leather, and smoke. Depending on how long the wine has been aged in oak it can have some very strong black pepper notes. I find French Malbec, aka Cot, to usually be lighter, more reserved in style, compared to the Argentine versions; it’s more solidly medium bodied, sometimes with more pronounced green notes, and less bold spice, as can be expected from the cooler growing climate. Usually it’s a bit player in Bordeaux blends but you can find some interesting single varietals from southern France.

Whites
  • Pinot Gris/Pinot Grigio

    People at the liquor store I used to work with called this “Cougar Juice” because many women of a certain age would drink this (lots of people I worked with were gross.) Pinot Gris and Pinot Grigio are the same grape, one is the French name the other, Italian, but both are applied to productions from around the world. Pinot Gris is generally used to describe a fruitier version while wines using the name Pinot Grigio tend to be a little greener and perhaps more mineral (like the ones coming out of Italy.) That being said, regardless of style Pinot Grigio is always light, crisp, and easy drinking. It’s super refreshing on a hot day but honestly, it’s not my favorite. That being said I almost always have at least some Pinot Grigio if I’m hosting a party because some people don’t really like Pinot Grigio, but nobody really hates Pinot Grigio.

  • Riesling

    For some reason Riesling has gotten a reputation amongst “people who know things about wine” as a secret star of the wine world. I suppose it’s because it got a bad reputation when the syrupy sweet versions were all everybody’s grandmother drank. Now, whenever I try to recommend Riesling to someone, their first reaction tends to be “Oh no, I don’t like sweet wine.”

    Let’s get one thing straight: Not all Riesling is sweet. When you see “wine-people” fawning over a Riesling they may be compelled to specify “A good, dry, Riesling.” Dry Rieslings from Germany will have the word “Trocken” on the label to signify them as dry. That being said, Rieslings can also make wonderful, sweet, dessert wines. If you’re looking for something on the sweeter side look for the word “Auselese” or “Spatlese” on the label. These words all, specifically, refer to how ripe the grapes were when they were picked, but ripeness denotes sweetness, so just go with it. Another cheat, although this one is not a hard and fast rule, if you’re wondering if a wine is sweet or not sweet, look at the alcohol content. Sugar gets eaten by yeast during fermentation and its what is turned into alcohol. The more alcohol in the wine, the fewer sugars will be left, resulting in a drier wine. If you see an ABV under 8%, that’s almost definitely a sweet wine, drier versions will usually fall upwards of 12%, with the semi-dry/semi sweet falling in between. An off-dry Riesling (aka one with a smidge of sweetness) is hands down my absolute favorite thing to eat with spicy thai food. They offer notes of baked golden apples with an undercurrent of salinity, and a robust acidity that will stand up to the strong flavors of the food. Dry Rieslings can tend to be a little greener than this, with more strong mineral notes and a sharp acidity, but the snobs are right, a great dry Riesling is something to be cherished.

  • Sauvignon Blanc

    If you want a fruity, dry, mineral, white wine, Sauvignon Blanc is your wine. Originally from France, Sauvignon Blanc has really taken hold in the New Zealand wine scene. New Zealand and American versions are defined by their grapefruit and passion fruit flavors. I would argue that American versions are slightly less intense than their New Zealand counterparts, but they are both going for the same profile. Depending on the quality, these wines will also have some stoniness or minerality to them to round out the complexity. French ones, such as those from Sancerre, in the south of France, are greener than their New World brethren, and although they often times have some of the quintessential grapefruit character, they also are accompanied by sharper citrus fruits, like lemon and lime, and can be straight up salty, waxy, and grungy (if they’re really good.) If you’re just starting to dip your toes into Sauvignon Blanc I would start with a quality bottle from New Zealand in the $12-15 range; if that turns out to be juicy and fruit forward, try a similarly priced bottle from the Loire region in France which will have more of those green notes. If you’re ready for something really different, plop down $30-$40 for a really good, single vineyard version from Sancerre and get ready for it to funk you up.

  • Chenin Blanc

    Chenin Blanc is a light-medium bodied white wine oftentimes with notes of white peach, honeysuckle and lemon. The richer flavors are usually cut with a fair amount of acidity, but the wines are a bit heftier than the light styles of Pinot Grigio and Sauvignon Blanc discussed above. The versions I find most often are Dry or Off-Dry (with a small amount of residual sugar.) Dry versions have a sharper acidity and can often have mineral notes of stone, but not quite salty. Chenin Blanc is a big big grape in South Africa’s wine country, and dry versions from quality producers are some of the best values in white wine. I love an off-dry Vouvray (a region in southern France) where the small amount of sugar in the wine bring out the richer notes from the floral honey suckle and the stone fruit without becoming heavy and juicy. Chenin Blanc is one of those wines that might be best found with a guiding hand as in America it’s still a bit off the beaten path (although there are amounts of it grown in California where the peach notes really shine thanks to the warmer growing temperatures) so I would ask the shop attendant for a good recommendation if you don’t know where to start.

  • Gewurztraminer

    Gewurztraminer is what we call an “aromatic white.” Riesling, Viognier, and Pinot Gris also fall into this category (among others.) All this means is that these wines have a chemical component called terpenes, which release particularly strong aromas; you will get lots of strong, floral, and fruit notes when you shove your nose into a glass of Gewurz and sniff. The grapes have a high concentration of sugar when ripe and are most commonly found in an off-dry style, if not a full on sweet dessert wine. Gewurtraminer does really well in cool climates (as does Riesling) so you will find a lot of it in Germany, Austria, and the Alsace region of France (which is right across the border from Germany and Austria.) Gewürztraminer also does really well in, perhaps surprisingly to you, upstate New York. The Finger Lakes region in New York State makes some of the best cool-climate varietal wines in the United States, and some of my personal favorites. If you find Riesling too acidic for your taste, try a Gewürztraminer, they have many of the same honey and cooked fruit notes, and pair equally well with spicy food.

  • Sémillon

    Sémillon has a similar body to Chardonnay without the tropic fruit notes warm weather expressions can have. I wouldn't call Sémillon acidic, although all wines have acid, but a rounder, sometimes waxier contender. Sémillon is often found in white Bordeaux blends and when blended with Sauvignon Blanc (which greatly ups the acid factor) you can get honey crisp apple and other round fruit flavors. Australia makes some interesting Sémillon as well, similar in style to their Chardonnays, without quite as much ripeness, and the quintessential waxiness running in the background.

    Sémillon really stars as the main component in a famous French dessert wine called Sauternes. Sauternes is produced when ripe Sémillon is infected with a fungus called botrytis, aka noble rot. The mold concentrates the sugary juices in the grapes such that when pressed, the gold juice is almost like a thin syrup. Top Sauternes can cost hundreds, if not thousands of dollars and can be aged for hundreds of years, in the right conditions. Foie gras is the classic pairing with Sauternes and definitely a choice if you want to go full-fancy style. As a vegetarian, I cannot speak to it personally, but I would say anything fatty, luscious and maybe a little stinky, like a washed-rind triple creme brie, or buttery funky blue cheese, would be delicious too.

  • Viognier

    I only recently started really getting into Viognier. It’s another aromatic white wine, with a body similar to a Chardonnay, but usually without strong oak aging. Viogniers have flavors of peach, flowers, yellow apples and are sometimes subject to a process called malolatic fermentation which can lead to fewer apple notes and some distinct creamy, milky, notes (it always reminds me of fruit-on-the-bottom peach yogurt.) Viognier is a low-acid grape and is found abundantly in the white whine blends in the Northern Rhone region of Southern France. Outside of France, Viognier has really only started to take hold in the last 25 years or so. Australia makes some really interesting Viogniers, and one of my favorites, where I first encountered those weird peach yogurt vibes, but the new hot kid on the street, getting all of the attention, is Viognier coming out of Virginia, of all places.

    I can’t talk about Viognier without mentioning Côte-Rôtie. Côte-Rôtie is a region in France but also refers to the famous blended red wine produced there. Côte-Rôtie is mostly Syrah but is always blended with an amount of Viognier (up to 20%.) The resulting wine is definitely a red wine which is interesting because red and white grapes are very rarely blended together, unless it’s to make a specific style of Rose. Viognier is used in Côte-Rôtie to mellow the Syrah and also enhance the wine’s color and aroma. Côte-Rôtie is what I would call an advanced level wine, because you pretty much can’t find a bottle for less than $50, if you’re lucky.

  • Chardonnay

    For some reason Chardonnay is such an incredibly polarizing grape; to hear people talk you’d think it was the cilantro of the wine world: you either love it or your hate it. I would argue that the reality is more nuanced, and that a lot of people who think they hate Chardonnay, can be persuaded otherwise.

    Chardonnay is about as full bodied as white wines come. They can be aged in oak, but unoaked varieties are becoming increasingly common (don’t tell Chablis: a region in Burgundy in France that has been making purely unoaked Chardonnay for centuries.) When people say they hate Chardonnay, generally what they mean is they hate big, oaky, buttery, California Chardonnay. The butteriness comes from a combination of malolactic fermentation which lends itself to creamy, milky, notes, and the oak aging which gives it an oiliness. This kind of oaked Chardonnay can be really intense. That coupled with notes of sweet, heavy, tropical fruits like Pineapple, Papapya, Mano, and baked Apple, sometimes without a strong acidic undertone, defines many of California's offerings which some people love but can also, apparently, incite violence in the hearts of many.

    This is just one kind of Chardonnay. Fly across the ocean to Burgundy (or just up the coast to Oregon, or south to Argentina…you get it) you’ll find plenty of other expressions of Chardonnay. Chablis is one example, and probably my personal favorite. Chablis, and other unoaked varieties are much fresher tasting than their heavy oaky cousins; they have notes of green apple, pear, unripe pit fruit, slate, and salt. The acidity in these wines is more pronounced which allows the wine to feel lighter. If your tastebuds are used to California Chardonnay, they might not even recognize unoaked Chardonnay as the same grape. Chablis can get expensive but try an unoaked offering from Oregon, Argentina, or Australia for something more cost effective, or look for a french Chardonnay with the words “Macon-Villages” on it, the villages level classification is a less prestigious one than the legally designated growing region of Chablis, but they almost never use oak, and clock in solidly under $15.
