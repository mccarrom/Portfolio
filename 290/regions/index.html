Wine around the world

Wine is grown all over the world, but mostly between the 30th and 50th parallels across the globe. These bands are home to the ideal climate for viticulture (aka grape growing.) On this page explore just a sampling of regions, and what they have to offer.

Italy

Italy is the largest producer of wine in the world, by volume. They mostly produce wines with indigenous grapes including the big ones: Sangiovese and Nebbiolo. White wine wise Italy is known for Pinot Grigio, most prolifically, but there are many other white Italian wines worth your attention. Some of my favorites include Gavi di Gavi, Vermentino di Sardegnia, and Verdichio. Italy, like France, labels their wines by where they are grown (appellation) as opposed to what grape is in the bottle (varietal.) With the appelation on the bottle, you, the cultured wine consumer is suppose to just know what grapes are grown there and make up the wine.

France

France is the second largest wine producing region in the world, many of the varietals grown in France compromise the 18 Noble Grapes discussed on the Grapes page. For centuries France defined the wine game, and the abundance of French grapes, world wide, is just one piece of proof of that.

Spain

Spain is the third largest wine growing region in the world, and the largest, if we're going by geographical area. Despite the size of its production, Spain doesn't have nearly the same recognition and/or reputation as Italy and France do, in the US. That's good news for the Spanish wine consumer though because it means that Spain is the place to go for VALUE in wine. If I'm spending less than $10 on a bottle of wine, you can bet it will be from Spain; and don't even get me started on Cava. Cava is Spanish sparkling wine; it is made in the same method as Champagne and therefore has the same fine, soft bubbles as Champagne, but uses indigenous grapes to Spain. Flavor wise, Cava and Champagne are pretty similar, much more so than Champagne and the well-known inexpensive dupe Prosecco. Cava is such a tiny fraction of the price of Champagne; for my birthday last year I had a top tier Cava from 10 years ago and it cost just over $60, Champagne of that quality would cost over $100. You have my permission to pillage your wine shop's Spanish section.

Portugal

Spain is rivaled for value in Europe only by Portugal. Portugal is so much more than Port, the sweet, fortified dessert wine it’s famous for. Picture this: i’s a 200 degree day with 500% humidity in the middle of July. You aren’t sure where your body ends and your chair begins the leather has fused so firmly to your skin. You are parched. Crack open a crispy, refreshing, slightly effervescent Vinho Verde from Portugal. You will chug that down in no time, and feel 1000% better. It won’t matter that you weren’t able to savor the wine either, Vinho Verde cost about $5 a bottle!

On the other end of the spectrum: if you like your reds full-bodied, fruit forward, with a hint of green pepper, or green bean earthy underpinning, Portugal is going to become your new best friend.

Germany

Germany makes so much more than Riesling, but their Rieslings are transcendent. Germany is very cold as far as wine-growing regions are concerned which means certain grapes are likely to flourish there. Riesling is the most well known followed by the recognizeable Gewurztraminer, which is only recognizeable because of the unpronounceability of its name. Within the last ten years Germany has begun producing some top tier Pinot Noir. To my palate it rivals good Burgundy, but is a touch smokier.

Austria

Gruner Veltliner and Blaufrankish are the two wines Austria is most well-known for. Gruner is a sharp, acidic, mineral white, that is super refreshing and perfect for roasted vegetables with brown caramelized bits. Blaufrankish is an indigenous red grape that is like if Pinot Noir and Gamay had a super funky baby. Most of the words in that sentence might not have meant anything to you, in which case, you need to be drinking more wine.

Greece

I really love Greek wine and wish more people drank it. Many of the reds, mostly made from indigenous grapes, can rival high quality Italian red wines, at a fraction of the price (and self-importance) but my true love is Greek white wines. You will see people (including myself) talk about minerality in wine. Usually, mineral notes are a result of the elements of the soil where the wine is grown, known as the terrior but some wines have a mineral salinity to them for a completely different, and fascinating reason. Greek wine grapes, in the Mediterranean, and others grown in similar circumstances, are at the mercy of the Atlantic Ocean. Ocean breezes blow salt onto the grapes, from the ocean, influencing the flavors of the final wine.

USA

Americans drink a lot of American wine. The United States is the fourth largest wine producing region in the world. It's hard to think that just over 40 years ago American wine, Californian particularly, was seen as a complete joke on the world's stage. There was a dramatic competition called "The Judgement of Paris" where California wines were tasted blind by a panel of experts, against the best wines France had to offer. Spoiler alert: California beat France for the top prize both in Reds (Cabernet Sauvignon Stags Leap Cellars) and Whites (Chardonnay from Chateau Montelena.) Now California is synonymous with quality American wine, but many of them come with the price tag to match.

If you like the California style of wine try exploring wines from Washington State, Argentina, and Chile. If you find California wines too intense I would suggest trying something from the Willamette Valley in Oregon, to start.

Fun fact: Wine is grown and produced in every single state in America. Yes, including Hawaii. Yes, even Alaska (there are two fully greenhoused vineyards in Alaska, who knew?!) The Finger Lakes in upstate New York offers wonderful German and Austrain varieties and Virginia is the hot, new item, gettinge everyone's attention.

Argentina

Argentina isn't all about Malbec. That being said, it's mostly about Malbec. Malbec put Argentine wine on the map in this country when Australian Shiraz was first falling out of favor. Americans wanted a medium to full bodied, fruit forward wine, that wasn't sweet per say, but wasn't tannic and intimidating either. Enter:Malbec.

If you're looking to branch out of Malbec, Argentina makes some great Syrah, Pinot Noir, and Cabernet Sauvignon in a style very similar to many Californian producers but at half of the price. On the white wine front you can find some bargain unoaked Chardonnay, but the real star of the white wine scene in Argentina is Torrontes, a light, acidic, salty, minerally, green wine that's perfect with lighter fare, or on a brutally hot day.

Chile

Chile and Argentina are competitors and I am far from unbiased in the fight. That being said, I would argue that Chile is a better value than Argentine wine nowadays. The styles are very similar. Although, if you're a fan of red wines with green flavors, Argentina doesn't really have anything to compare to Chilean Carménère. Chilean wines are cheap, to the point that many bargain producers that use to source their juice from American vineyards, now source it from Chile; and nobody noticed.

australia

Australian wine got such a bad name from the saturation of a brand that shall remain nameless. Now, aside from Shiraz, which they are still doing wonderfully, they are churning out some of the best Rhone style wines outside of Rhone. Australian Winemakers are also jumping on the "natural wine" train full force and you can find some really funky experimental bottles, if you're brave enough.

South Africa

Chenin Blanc and Pinotage define South African Wine. Pinotage is a failed attempt to breed a heartier Pinot Noir grape that could survive the hot summers of South Africa. I say failed, but Pinotage really is more of a happy accident. It's slightly softer, and fruitier, than Pinot Noir, and a wine that put South Africa on the mad. As Malbec is to Argentina, and Shiraz is to Australia, Pinotage is to South Africa.
